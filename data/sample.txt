The History and Workings of Lighthouses

A lighthouse is a tower with a bright light at the top, built on or near the coast to guide ships and warn them of dangerous areas. For thousands of years, lighthouses have helped sailors find safe passage at night and in poor weather.

The most famous lighthouse of the ancient world was the Pharos of Alexandria, built on a small island off the coast of Egypt around 280 BCE. It stood well over 100 metres tall and was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. A fire burned at its top at night, and a polished bronze mirror reflected the light far out to sea. The Pharos guided ships for over 1,500 years before earthquakes finally destroyed it.

Early lighthouses burned wood or coal. The light was weak and smoky, and it could only be seen from a short distance. The biggest change came in the early 1820s when a French engineer named Augustin-Jean Fresnel invented a new kind of lens. The Fresnel lens uses a series of concentric rings of glass to bend and concentrate light into a single powerful beam. With a Fresnel lens, even a small flame could be seen from many kilometres away. This invention transformed lighthouses across the world and saved countless lives.

Lighthouses use distinct flashing patterns so that sailors can tell them apart. Each lighthouse has its own signature, called a "character". One might flash once every five seconds, while another flashes twice and then pauses. By matching the pattern they see to a chart, navigators can identify exactly which lighthouse they are looking at and therefore know their position.

For most of history, lighthouses were operated by keepers who lived on site. The keeper's job was demanding and often lonely. They had to keep the lamp burning all night, polish the lens, wind the clockwork that rotated the light, and sound a fog signal in poor visibility. Many lighthouses were in remote and dangerous places, and keepers sometimes went weeks without seeing another person.

Today almost all lighthouses are automated. Electric lights, GPS navigation, and remote monitoring have removed the need for resident keepers. Many old lighthouses have been preserved as historic landmarks or turned into museums and guest houses. While modern ships rely mostly on satellite navigation, lighthouses remain a trusted backup and a powerful symbol of safety and guidance.
